## Design Context

### Users
Everyone is welcome — there is no primary user profile. Gestura serves hearing people who want to connect with deaf family, friends, or colleagues; deaf and hard-of-hearing people engaging with the app's community; students of all ages learning BIM for the first time; and people who are just curious about sign language.

Users open the app in a motivated but relaxed mindset — not under pressure, not in crisis. They want to feel welcomed and make progress. The subject matter (sign language, bridging communities) carries real emotional weight: connection, empathy, belonging.

The job to be done: learn BIM through structured, satisfying sessions — and feel the bidirectional bridge between signers and non-signers being built with every lesson.

### Brand Personality
Three words: **warm, communal, joyful**

Not warm like a spa. Warm like a community center where everyone already knows you're welcome. Communal like a shared project, not a leaderboard competition. Joyful in the small moments — completing a quiz, unlocking a badge, recognizing a sign for the first time.

The tone is educational without being institutional. Structured without being rigid. Playful without being childish. The learning is the point; the delight is how you get there.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Primary theme: dark mode** (most users default to it; the dark navy palette is already established)

Visual tone: expressive and warm — not cold, not clinical, not the sterile EdTech look, not Duolingo-green-and-aggressive. The app's subject matter is gestural and visual, so the interface should echo that: kinetic energy, expressive shapes, motion that feels like movement rather than decoration.

Anti-references:
- NOT generic EdTech (cookie-cutter icon grids, the same blue/white hospital-clean aesthetic)
- NOT aggressive gamification (Duolingo-style guilt trips and harsh reds)
- NOT corporate SaaS (Inter everywhere, card grids, sidebar stripe accents)
- NOT "AI slop" dark mode (glowing cyan/purple neon on black, glassmorphism cards)

The established palette is fixed — dark navy (`#0F0F1A`, `#1A1A2E`, `#16162A`), indigo primary (`#6366F1`), purple secondary (`#8B5CF6`), pink accent (`#EC4899`). Work within it; do not replace it.

**Typography direction:** Replace Plus Jakarta Sans. The new pairing should be:
- **Display/headings**: Familjen Grotesk — geometric but warm, has character without being a reflex pick, feels like something printed on a community event flyer rather than a SaaS landing page
- **Body**: Lexend — designed from research on reading accessibility and comfortable legibility, appropriate for an app that must work for everyone including people who may have reading differences. Not in common EdTech use.

### Design Principles
1. **Warmth before efficiency** — every screen should feel like an invitation, not a task list. Generous spacing around content, friendly tone in labels and empty states.
2. **Motion that echoes gesture** — animations should have kinetic weight, like a hand completing a sign: decisive, smooth, and purposeful. No bouncing, no elastic snap.
3. **Community over competition** — gamification (XP, streaks, leaderboards) should read as celebration, not pressure. Reward loops feel like sharing progress, not scoring against others.
4. **Delight in small moments** — quiz feedback, achievement unlocks, lesson completions are the emotional high points. Give them room to breathe and feel satisfying.
5. **Inclusive by default** — high contrast, clear type hierarchy, no functional information conveyed by color alone. The app serves the deaf community too; accessibility is not an afterthought.
